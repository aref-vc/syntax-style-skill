# The revision pass

Two passes. First the sentence, then the seams between sentences. Change only the sentences doing no work; do not rewrite everything.

## Pass 1: sentence level

Read the passage aloud, or hear it in your head. Then go line by line.

1. **Rhythm in both directions.** Are the sentences all about the same length? Find the longest stretch of sameness and break it. If they are all *long*, build a short one for contrast. If they are all *short and choppy*, combine several onto one base clause with free modifiers. Choppy is a failure too, not a fix.
2. **Long sentences: free or bound?** For each long sentence, check whether its length comes from free modifiers hanging off a clean base clause (good) or from nested "which/that" clauses and stacked prepositions (bound-modifier glut, bad). Rebuild the bad ones on a clean base. Do not chop them into fragments.
3. **Openers.** Does every sentence start with the subject? Open one with a modifier, a prepositional phrase, or an inversion.
4. **Buried verbs.** Scan for "-tion", "-ment", "-ance" nouns and "make/do/have" plus a noun. Restore the verb.
5. **Intensifier clutter.** Delete very, really, actually, quite, extremely. Check each remaining adjective: information or just heat? Watch -ly pile-ups.
6. **The "and" chains.** Are the joined items equal? If yes, a parallel series or sustained coordination is fine, and is the right closer for a narrative. If one is subordinate, demote it with because/although/when.
7. **The triad reflex.** Count your three-item lists and three-clause sentences. Is three the real number, or a habit? Prefer the pair and antithesis where the content has two sides.
8. **Misplaced modifiers.** Is each modifier next to the word it means?

## Pass 2: paragraph cohesion

9. **Known-to-new.** Does each sentence start with something the reader already has and end on the new thing? Reorder so the new lands at the end and becomes the next sentence's start.
10. **Carry ideas forward.** Use a demonstrative ("That decision...", "This is why...") to compress a whole prior sentence into the next one's subject.
11. **Key terms.** Repeat the term instead of swapping synonyms.
12. **Length as weave.** Across the paragraph, is the sentence length varied enough to read smooth rather than lumpy?
13. **One change of mood.** Is it all declarative? Consider one question or direct address where a beat is needed.

## Pass 3: slop scan (fast)

- Reveal stacks / one-sentence paragraphs that should be combined
- The triad reflex (count them)
- "It's not just X, it's Y" and "not only ... but also"
- Em dashes (more than one per paragraph is usually too many)
- Throat-clearing openers ("It is important to note", "In today's world")
- Hedge stacks ("might possibly somewhat potentially")
- The impersonal passive ("It was decided that...") where an agent should be named

## The discipline

After each change, reread the sentence in context. A fix that improves one sentence can break the rhythm of the three around it. The unit of revision is the paragraph, not the sentence.

Two overrides:
- Before cutting a repetition or a short fragment, confirm it is accidental. Deliberate repetition and a landed short sentence are the goal.
- Before shortening, ask whether the real fix is a clean *long* sentence built from free modifiers. Shorter is not automatically better.
