# Principles

Ten ideas carry the book. They are weighted the way the book weights them, which is not where an untrained instinct lands. The book spends far more pages on the long, well-built sentence than on the short punchy one, and it treats the triad as one option among many, not the default.

## 1. Syntax is meaning, not packaging

How a sentence is arranged changes what it says. "Work was his life" and "His life was his work" point at different things. Before you choose words, choose a shape. The shape is a decision.

## 2. Build from a base clause, then branch

A sentence has a kernel: a subject and a verb, sometimes an object. The artful sentence is that kernel plus free modifiers attached to it. The same model produces a two-word sentence and a sixty-word one. Find the kernel, write it clean, then decide what to attach and where.

## 3. The long cumulative sentence is the signature move, not a failure

The book's center of gravity is the right-branching cumulative sentence: a grammatically complete base clause followed by a run of free modifiers that elaborate it. This is the workhorse of good modern prose, fiction and nonfiction. It reads as natural accumulation, the logic of addition. A flat, choppy passage of four short sentences is often best fixed by building *one* cumulative sentence, not by leaving it chopped. Do not assume shorter is better. Frequently the artful version is longer.

Branches attach in three directions, and the direction carries meaning:
- **Right (cumulative):** base clause first, then modifiers pile on. Lands the point, then elaborates. The default.
- **Left:** modifiers first, base clause delayed. Builds suspense; the main clause arrives as an almost inevitable conclusion. Powerful but front-heavy if overlong.
- **Mid:** the base clause is interrupted. Tightens and compresses from both ends; signals a writer who organized the thought before modifying it. Costs clarity; use sparingly.

## 4. Free modifiers, not bound modifiers

This is the distinction that separates a rich sentence from a lumpy one. **Free (nonrestrictive)** modifiers are set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses; they hang off the base clause and can be moved or removed without breaking it. **Bound (restrictive)** modifiers are woven into the grammar: nested "which" and "that" clauses, stacked prepositional phrases ("the report on the results of the study of the effects of...").

A long sentence built from free modifiers stays clear. A long sentence choked with bound modifiers is the real villain, the "prepositional glut." When a long sentence fails, the cure is almost never "make it short." It is "rebuild it on a clean base clause and hang free modifiers off that."

## 5. Match the structure to the job

Each structure is good at one thing. Reach for the one whose job matches your intent.

| You want | Reach for |
|---|---|
| A point to land hard, by contrast with what precedes it | a short sentence |
| Natural accumulation of detail | a right-branching cumulative sentence |
| Suspense, a delayed and inevitable conclusion | left-branching |
| Two things weighed against each other | balance or antithesis (a pair) |
| One idea ranked under another | subordination |
| To rename or define in place | an appositive |
| Emphasis on an unusual element | inversion / a marked opener |
| Resolution, an unhurried close to a narrative | sustained coordination |

The full set, with sub-types, is in `structures.md`.

## 6. Variation is the engine, and it cuts both ways

The most common failure is sameness: every sentence the same length, every one opening with the subject. But the over-correction is also a failure. A page of clipped three-word sentences is just as monotonous as a page of long ones, and it loses the cumulative power the book prizes. Vary length in both directions. Set a genuinely long sentence next to a genuinely short one and let the contrast do the work.

## 7. Parallelism is balance and antithesis, not a triad reflex

Parallelism means saying like things in like ways. Its core is the **pair**: two weighted elements, often in **antithesis** (set against each other) or **chiasmus** (mirror-image order). It also includes series of any length, anaphora (repeated openings), and parallelism that scaffolds a very long sentence so it stays navigable. The three-item triad is one length among many. Reaching for three every time is a tell, not a technique. Use the number the content needs: two when there are two, five when there are five.

## 8. Short sentences earn their power by contrast

A short sentence is loud because of what surrounds it, not in itself. Dropped after three long sentences it lands like a verdict. Stacked five in a row it is just choppy. The short sentence is also the nucleus that later chapters expand into branching sentences. Treat it as a tool for contrast and finality, not as the house style.

## 9. Coordination is the natural closer

For endings, especially narrative ones, sustained coordination ("and ... and ...") beats subordination. It is free of the weighing and ranking that subordination imposes, so it reads as resolution and aesthetic order rather than analysis. The effect can run across several closing paragraphs. "And" is never merely additive; it signals continuous, ongoing, accumulating action.

## 10. Form can enact meaning, in any direction

A sentence can perform what it describes, and not only by speeding up. The full range: lengthening units enact growth or accumulation; shortening units enact diminishment or extinction; a held-back main verb (periodic structure) enacts suspense and arrival; fragments enact disorder or shock; repetition enacts insistence or monotony; sustained coordination enacts continuity; parataxis enacts a scene rushing past. Acceleration is one direction among many. Use it where the content offers an obvious shape; do not force it.

## What good looks like

A passage that applies these reads as if the writer made choices: some sentences genuinely long and well-built on a clean base, some genuinely short for contrast, openings varied, parallelism that favors the pair, repetition where it earns its place, and each sentence handing the reader forward. Nothing is ornamental, and nothing is chopped just to look brisk.
