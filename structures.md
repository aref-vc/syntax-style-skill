# Structures

The 14 moves, in the book's order. Each entry: the job it does, its range from simple to elaborate, the sub-types worth knowing, and a worked example written for this skill. The examples deliberately span lengths; several "after" versions are longer than their "before", because the artful version often is.

## Quick pick: goal to structure

## 1. Short sentences

**Job:** finality and contrast. A short sentence is loud because of the long ones around it, not in itself. It also serves as the nucleus that branching sentences expand.

**Range:** from a bare two-word kernel to the short sentence used as a topic sentence, a pivot, or an abrupt climax inside a paragraph of longer ones. The book's lesson is that the power lives in the *relation* to surrounding length, not in shortness itself.

**Sub-types by verb energy:** `be`-equations (lowest energy, aphoristic), linking verbs (became, seemed), intransitives (workhorse), transitives (highest, when the object carries new information). End-stress: English puts the news at the end, so a back-referring pronoun subject is fine; load the second half.

```
flat (all short, choppy):  The board met. They reviewed it. They were unimpressed. They voted no.
crafted (long then short):  The board spent the morning on it, three rounds of questions, two abstentions, a long argument about timing. Then they voted no.
```

## 2. Noun phrases

**Job:** name precisely, pack detail into one unit, control abstraction. A noun phrase need not contain a noun: a gerund, an infinitive, or a whole clause can fill the slot.

**Range and sub-types:**
- Noun as modifier of a noun ("night sky" tightens "the sky at night").
- Gerund phrases (suggest ongoing process), infinitive phrases, clauses as nouns ("what the market does next").
- The **noun-as-fragment**, used deliberately and often by good writers, sometimes in a graduated run of increasing length.
- The **climactic appositive** that delays the real subject to the end of the sentence.
- The **noun series / catalog**, best rounded off with a final item longer than the rest so it does not end flat.

**Failure mode:** the noun dragging stacked bound relative clauses. The rule is not "use fewer noun phrases" but "do not build them with bound modifiers."

```
flat:     The thing where you decide which features to build is the part that is hard.
crafted:  Choosing what to build, and what to leave unbuilt, is the hard part.
```

## 3. Verb phrases

**Job:** supply drive. The verb is where the sentence moves; weak prose hides its verbs inside nouns.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Finite verbs in series**, whose order carries meaning (escalation, sequence).
- **Nonfinite verbals** that import a whole mini-predicate cheaply: present participles (ongoing action), gerunds (process), infinitives (purpose, and chains that become the major stretch of a sentence), past participles (descriptive, the essence is passivity not pastness).
- **Voice as a tool, not a sin.** The passive legitimately shifts topic, relocates the old subject to the end for expansion, drops an unimportant agent, and stitches cohesion across sentences. The failure is the *impersonal* agentless passive that hides responsibility ("It was decided that..."), not the passive itself.

```
flat:     There was a reduction in costs as a result of the team's optimization of the pipeline.
crafted:  The team optimized the pipeline, and costs fell.
```

## 4. Adjectives and adverbs

**Job:** texture and precision, and the most common source of clutter.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Attributive** (before the noun), **predicative** (after a linking verb), **appositive** (pulled out after the noun, set off by punctuation, often in pairs, which gives them weight).
- Adjective + prepositional phrase ("heavy with rain") elevates register.
- The isolated final adjective lands with finality.
- **Adverb mobility** is the core lesson: the same adverb can open, follow the subject, sit mid-clause, or close, and the choice tunes emphasis and cohesion. An opening adverb often ties a sentence to the one before it.
- Cut the intensifiers that carry no information: very, really, actually, quite, extremely. Watch -ly excess; it sets up a sing-song.

```
flat:     It was a really very important and quite significant decision for the company.
crafted:  The decision was irreversible, and everyone in the room knew it.
```

## 5. Prepositions

**Job:** fix relation. Harold Whitehall's five: **location, direction, association, agency, time.**

**Range and sub-types:**
- Objects can be nouns, possessives, gerunds ("by looking"), adverbs ("until now"), or whole clauses ("under whatever it was").
- A single preposition can take a series of objects, the last one expanded into a long elaboration.
- Phrases stack in parallel runs and can sit in several positions; place each next to the word it modifies, or it attaches to the wrong one.
- Used deliberately, a pile-up of directional phrases enacts motion. Ending a sentence with a preposition is fine.

**Failure mode:** "of" overuse makes prose lead-footed. "The wrath of God" can become "God's wrath," but weigh the cost to rhythm before you cut.

```
flat:     We need to make a decision about the matter of the budget by the end of the week.
crafted:  By Friday, we settle the budget.
```

## 6. Conjunctions and coordination

**Job:** accumulate, balance, and resolve. Each conjunction has a force: "and" signals continuous, ongoing action, not mere addition; "but/yet" pivots; "so/for" gives cause or result.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Parataxis** (clauses side by side, often connected by semicolons, dashes, or colons) reads fast and urgent, or, in another context, relaxed and unhurried. Works best with short kernels.
- **Polysyndeton** (and ... and ... and) builds rhythm and abundance.
- **Asyndeton** (connectors dropped) reads abrupt.
- **Correlatives** (both ... and, not only ... but also) hold a pair in suspension until the second half resolves it.

**The key claim:** for closing a passage, especially a narrative, sustained coordination beats subordination. It carries no ranking or analysis, so it reads as resolution and aesthetic order. The effect can run across several final paragraphs.

```
flat:     We started in a garage. We grew to ten people. Then we passed a thousand customers.
crafted:  We started in a garage, and we grew to ten, and then to a thousand customers, and the garage we could not afford to leave became the office we cannot quite believe is ours.
```

## 7. Dependent clauses

**Job:** rank one idea beneath another. Two families: **relative clauses** (who, which, that, often omitted: "the book [that] I read") and **subordinate clauses** (because, although, when, while, if).

**Range and sub-types:**
- Short adverbial openers ("If she stumbles, ...").
- Correlative frames: "just as ... so ...", "the more ... the more ...".
- **Noun clauses** filling subject or object slots, including the terse aphoristic kind that implies a question and answers it ("What the team needs is time").

**The caveat the book insists on:** the main/subordinate split is structural, not a ranking of importance. Vital meaning often lives in a dependent clause. Do not treat "subordinate" as "throwaway."

```
flat:     The vendor missed the deadline. The launch slipped two weeks. We still made our number.
crafted:  Although the vendor missed the deadline and the launch slipped two weeks, we still made our number for the quarter.
```

## 8. Sentence openers and inversion

**Job:** emphasis by word order. English puts known information first and the new last, so moving an element changes the stress.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Adverbial openers** (a quarter of all sentences): time, place, manner, often stacked ("Early that spring, quietly, the policy changed").
- **Verbal openers:** participial and infinitive phrases.
- **Inversion proper:** fronted object ("Critics he ignored"), expletive "there"/"it" (which postpone the subject so it can be amplified), negative-triggered inversion ("Nor was there any warning").
- Four payoffs: **emphasis** (front the stressed element), **amplification** (front a short element so the subject lands where it can be expanded), **cohesion** (put the linking parts of adjacent sentences next to each other), and **literary flair**.

```
flat:     The mistake became obvious only after the third outage.
crafted:  Only after the third outage did the mistake become obvious.
```

## 9. Free modifiers and branching sentences

**Job:** build a long, clear sentence by accumulation. This is the heart of the book. A free modifier is set off from a grammatically complete base clause and can be moved or removed without breaking it.

**The fillers:** prepositional phrases, present and past participial phrases, dependent clauses, **nominative absolutes** (a noun plus a participle, carrying its own subject: "her hands trembling"), appositive noun and adjective clusters.

**The three directions** (built on one base clause, "I watched him"):
- **Right / cumulative:** "I watched him, wondering who he was, my hand already on the door." The workhorse. Natural accumulation. In nonfiction keep the right branches short, one or two; in fiction they can run loose.
- **Left:** "Sensing a rival, I watched him." Suspense, delayed subject.
- **Mid:** "I, sensing a rival, watched him." Tightens from both ends; use sparingly.

**The clarity rule:** if a branch (especially a participial phrase) could attach to the wrong noun, keep it next to the noun it means. Nominative absolutes are more positionally free.

```
flat:     The founder walked in. She was late. She was underslept. She carried three coffees.
crafted:  The founder walked in late and underslept, three coffees balanced in two hands, her laptop bag slipping off one shoulder, already apologizing to no one in particular.
```

## 10. The appositive

**Job:** rename, define, exemplify, or contrast, in place, without "which is". The free (comma- or dash-set-off) appositive is the stylistic instrument; the bound one ("her cousin Oliver") just identifies.

**Range and sub-types:**
- Appositive nouns and **appositive adjectives** (pulled out after the noun for weight).
- **Reiteration:** repeat the principal word to create a fresh peg for new detail.
- The **end-position appositive** that delays the momentous words to the close of the sentence, the chapter's most dramatic move.
- **Chains**, where a noun inside one appositive becomes the principal for the next.
- Long cumulative appositive series that build a portrait with almost no syntactic strain.

```
flat:     Our head of design, who is the person who built the original brand system, is leaving.
crafted:  Our head of design is leaving, the person who drew the first logo on a napkin and every version since.
```

## 11. Interrogative, imperative, exclamatory

**Job:** change the mood inside otherwise declarative prose. All three make a demand: a question asks for an answer, a command for an action, an exclamation for shared feeling.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Questions:** the genuine question that raises suspense; the **leading question** that embeds its answer; the **rhetorical question** that announces the answer is already reached; the "Or is it?" challenge that undercuts an assumption.
- **Imperatives:** the strong command, the mild essayistic directive ("Notice that..."), the "let us" invitation.
- **Exclamatives:** often built by inversion ("What a mess this is") and frequently written without an exclamation point.

**Rule:** the force comes from rarity. One question or command where the reader needs a beat; not a page of them.

```
flat:     Many teams are paying for dashboards that nobody actually looks at.
crafted:  Paying for dashboards no one reads? We were too.
```

## 12. Parallelism

**Job:** weigh like things in like ways, and build rhythm. The center is the **pair**, not the triad.

**Range and sub-types:**
- **Balance:** two equally weighted, symmetrically built halves.
- **Antithesis:** a pair set in contrast ("cheap to start, expensive to leave").
- **Chiasmus:** mirror-image order ("we shape our tools, and our tools shape us").
- **Series of any length**, including long parallel runs that scaffold a sentence so it stays navigable, and "if ... then" alternation.
- **Anaphora** (repeated opening) and tail repetition for rhythm.
- **The climactic break:** build a tight parallel pattern, then snap it with a short non-parallel line.

**Watch the triad reflex.** Three is one length among many. Reaching for three every time is the tell. Use the number the content needs.

```
flat (forced triad):  It's fast, it's secure, and it's affordable.
crafted (antithesis pair):  Powerful enough for the experts who demand it, simple enough for the rest of us who don't want to think about it.
```

## 13. Cohesion

**Job:** make a paragraph read as one piece. A full toolkit, not a single trick.

**The devices:**
- **Known-to-new:** start a sentence with what the reader holds, end on the new thing, then make that the next sentence's start.
- **Conjunction openers:** "But" pivots against a whole preceding block; "And" accumulates.
- **Demonstratives that carry a whole idea forward:** "That" or "this" can compress an entire prior sentence into one pointing word.
- **Repeated key terms** (not synonym churn, which breaks the thread).
- **Parallel openings** that make a paragraph feel sprung from one source.
- **Sentence-length variety:** sentences all the same length read lumpy; varied lengths weave smooth. Length variation is itself a cohesion device.

```
disjointed:  We rebuilt the pipeline. Latency was the issue. Customers had complained for months.
cohesive:    Customers had complained about latency for months. That latency, it turned out, lived in the pipeline. So the pipeline is what we rebuilt.
```

## 14. Syntactic symbolism

**Job:** make the sentence's shape perform its meaning. The highest move, and far broader than "speed up."

**The mechanisms, in many directions:**
- **Increase:** units that grow longer enact accumulation, growth, a rising level.
- **Attrition:** units that grow shorter, ending in clipped monosyllables, enact diminishment or extinction.
- **Suspense (periodic):** hold the main verb to the end, behind left branches, to enact anticipation and arrival.
- **Fragmentation:** broken units enact shock, disorder, or splintered experience.
- **Repetition:** a word or structure echoed enacts insistence, monotony, or a cycle.
- **Coordination:** sustained "and" enacts continuity.
- **Parataxis:** clauses run together enact a scene rushing past.

Use it where the content offers an obvious shape. The same shape does not carry a fixed meaning; it is local and occasional.

```
plain:     The approval process kept getting more complicated and slower over time.
symbolic (increase):  Step by step, approval by approval, sign-off stacked on sign-off, the process grew until it could no longer move.
```
