# Kill list

Patterns that make prose flat, foggy, or machine-generated, each paired with the structural move that fixes it. The fix is never "add more words" and, just as important, never "make it shorter" by reflex. It is "reach for the right structure".

The order is book-first: the craft failures the book targets, then the modern AI-flatness tells.

## From the book: the craft failures

### 1. Monotone (every sentence the same length and shape)
The central failure. Subject-verb-object, subject-verb-object, all about the same length. No rhythm.
**Fix:** vary length in both directions. Build one genuinely long cumulative sentence and set a short one against it. Open one sentence with a modifier instead of the subject. → `structures.md` 1, 8, 9.

### 2. Choppiness (the over-correction)
The opposite failure, and the one an AI trying to "tighten" usually commits: a page of clipped three- and four-word sentences. It is just as monotonous as #1 and it throws away the cumulative power the book prizes.
**Fix:** combine the fragments onto one clean base clause with free modifiers. Often the artful version is *longer*, not shorter. → 9, 6.

### 3. Bound-modifier glut
The real reason long sentences fail: a noun dragging nested "which" and "that" clauses, or stacked prepositional phrases ("the report on the results of the study of the effects of..."). Length is not the problem; the bound modifiers are.
**Fix:** rebuild on a clean base clause and hang *free* (comma-set, removable) modifiers off it. Do not chop into short sentences. → 2, 9.

### 4. Nominalization fog (verbs hidden inside nouns)
"The implementation of the optimization led to a reduction in latency." The real verbs are buried, so nothing moves.
**Fix:** restore the verbs. "We optimized it and latency dropped." → 3.

### 5. The impersonal passive
The agentless passive that hides who acted: "It was decided that..." The book defends the passive in general; this specific use is the failure.
**Fix:** name the agent, or switch to active. Keep the passive when the agent truly does not matter or for cohesion. → 3.

### 6. The "and ... and ... and" drone (when ideas are not equal)
Coordination used for ideas of unequal weight. Note: sustained "and" is *correct* for narrative resolution, so judge by purpose.
**Fix:** if the items are equal, keep the coordination or make a parallel series; if one is subordinate, demote it with because/although/when. → 6, 7, 12.

### 7. Adjective and adverb clutter
Intensifiers that carry nothing: very, really, actually, quite, extremely. -ly excess that sets up a sing-song.
**Fix:** cut intensifiers; replace adjective-plus-vague-noun with a precise word. Consider an appositive adjective for weight. → 4.

### 8. Misplaced modifiers
A free modifier or prepositional phrase attached to the wrong word.
**Fix:** put it next to the word it means. → 5, 9.

### 9. All declarative, no variation in mood
Page after page of statements; the reader is never asked or addressed.
**Fix:** one question or one direct command where a beat is needed. → 11.

### 10. Synonym churn
Calling one thing by four names across a paragraph to avoid repeating, which breaks the thread.
**Fix:** repeat the key term, or carry the idea forward with a demonstrative ("that ..."). → 13.

## Modern AI-flatness tells

### 11. The triad reflex
Everything in threes because three sounds authoritative: three adjectives, three clauses, three-item lists, regardless of content. The book explicitly treats the triad as one length among many.
**Fix:** use the number the content needs. Prefer the **pair** and **antithesis**. Two when there are two, five when there are five. → 12.

### 12. The reveal stack / deck cadence
One short sentence per line, each its own paragraph, building to a "point." Reads as a slide deck.
**Fix:** combine into real sentences with coordination and branching. Keep at most one deliberate one-line beat. → 6, 9.

### 13. "It's not just X, it's Y"
The negation-then-elevation template, and "not only ... but also," "more than just."
**Fix:** state the claim directly, or rename it with an appositive. "It's not just a tool, it's a platform" → "The tool became a platform." → 1, 10.

### 14. Em-dash overuse
The dash as an all-purpose connector, several per paragraph.
**Fix:** most can become a comma-set appositive, a colon, or two sentences. Keep the dash for a genuine interruption, rarely. → 10.

### 15. Throat-clearing openers
"It is important to note that," "In today's world," "When it comes to." The sentence starts before it starts.
**Fix:** delete the opener and front the subject. → 8.

### 16. Hedge stacks
"It seems that this might possibly be somewhat of a potential issue."
**Fix:** make the claim, or drop it. One hedge maximum if the uncertainty is real. → 1.

## The two tests before you cut

1. **Is this repetition deliberate?** Repetition that builds rhythm or insistence is a tool, not an error. Kill accidental sameness; keep the chosen move.
2. **Am I cutting to a real improvement, or just shortening?** Shorter is not automatically better. If the fix is a clean long sentence built from free modifiers, write that. Do not chop a good idea into a stack of fragments.
