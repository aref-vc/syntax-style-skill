# Before and after

Ten pairs. Each is a plain version of the kind written by default, then the same idea rebuilt with a named move, then the reason it works. Each is tagged by **length** (short or elaborate) and **topic** (business or marketing).

Notice what this set does *not* do: it does not chop everything into short sentences, and it does not resolve everything into a triad. Several "after" versions are longer than their "before", because the artful version often is. The first six are shown in the README image.

## In the visual

### 1. Choppy to cumulative — elaborate, business

> **Before:** The founder walked in. She was late. She was underslept. She was carrying three coffees.
>
> **After:** The founder walked in late and underslept, three coffees balanced in two hands, her laptop bag slipping off one shoulder, already apologizing to no one in particular.

*Move: right-branching cumulative sentence (§9).* Four clipped sentences become one base clause with free modifiers trailing off it. The fix for choppy prose is usually a longer, well-built sentence, not a shorter one.

### 2. Antithesis, not a triad — short, marketing

> **Before:** Our platform is fast, it's secure, and it's also affordable.
>
> **After:** Powerful enough for the experts who demand it, simple enough for the rest of us who would rather not think about it.

*Move: antithesis, a balanced pair (§12).* The reflex is three flat claims. A weighted pair set in contrast carries more and sounds less like a checklist.

### 3. Left-branching for suspense — elaborate, business

> **Before:** We cut our price. We did it after losing three deals in one quarter to a competitor who undercut us.
>
> **After:** After losing three deals in a single quarter to a rival who undercut us on every line, we cut our price.

*Move: left-branching (§9, §8).* The circumstances build first; the decision lands at the end as the almost inevitable conclusion. The shape delivers the logic.

### 4. Cumulative with an absolute — elaborate, marketing

> **Before:** Our app tracks your habits. It sends you reminders. It shows your progress. It keeps you motivated over time.
>
> **After:** The app tracks your habits and nudges you when you slip, a quiet mirror held up each morning, its streak counter ticking, until the habit no longer needs the app at all.

*Move: right-branching cumulative with an appositive and a nominative absolute (§9, §10).* One base clause carries an appositive ("a quiet mirror") and an absolute ("its streak counter ticking"), then lands the payoff. Four flat sentences could not build this arc.

### 5. Syntactic symbolism, increase — short, marketing

> **Before:** The approval process kept getting more complicated and slower over time until it barely worked.
>
> **After:** Step by step, approval by approval, sign-off stacked on sign-off, the process grew until it could no longer move.

*Move: syntactic symbolism (§14).* The accreting parallel phrases enact the pile-up they describe. The sentence gets heavier as the process does. Symbolism here is *increase*, not acceleration.

### 6. Short by contrast — elaborate to short, business

> **Before:** After a long process that involved many stakeholders and several rounds of review, the proposal was ultimately rejected by the committee.
>
> **After:** Months of review, a dozen stakeholders, three rounds of edits, two redesigns. The committee killed it.

*Move: long-then-short (§1).* The short sentence is loud only because the long one precedes it. Its power is the contrast, not the brevity. The opening list runs to four items on purpose, to break the three-beat habit.

## The rest

### 7. Coordination as closer — elaborate, business

> **Before:** We started in a garage. We grew to ten people. Then we passed a thousand customers. Now we have a real office.
>
> **After:** We started in a garage, and we grew to ten, and then to a thousand customers, and the garage we could not afford to leave became an office we cannot quite believe is ours.

*Move: sustained coordination (§6).* For a narrative close, coordination beats subordination: it carries no ranking or analysis, so it reads as resolution. "And" here signals continuous, accumulating motion, not mere addition.

### 8. Restore the verb — short, business

> **Before:** The implementation of the automation resulted in a significant reduction of manual processing time.
>
> **After:** We automated the workflow and cut manual processing time in half.

*Move: verb phrase (§3).* "Implementation" and "reduction" are verbs hiding inside nouns. Free them and the sentence moves.

### 9. Cohesion, carry the idea forward — elaborate, business

> **Before:** We rebuilt the billing system. Customers complained about invoices. Churn rose in Q2. Finance flagged the cause.
>
> **After:** Churn rose through Q2. Finance traced it to the invoices, which customers simply could not read. That confusion is what the billing rebuild was meant to end.

*Move: cohesion (§13).* Each sentence ends on its new idea and the next picks it up; "that confusion" compresses a whole prior sentence into the next one's subject.

### 10. Cut the template — short, marketing

> **Before:** It's not just a note-taking app, it's a second brain that transforms the way you think.
>
> **After:** The note-taking app quietly became the place its users think, not just the place they store.

*Move: appositive contrast (§10, kill-list §13).* The "not just X, it's Y" template is replaced by a direct claim with a balanced closing contrast.

## Reading these

The "after" versions vary length on purpose: some are long and built on a clean base clause, some are short for contrast. Parallelism shows up as pairs and antithesis more than as triads. Symbolism appears as increase, not only acceleration. None of the long ones is long because of nested clauses; each is long because free modifiers hang off a clear base. That is the whole discipline: not shorter, not three-at-a-time, but the structure the meaning asks for.
